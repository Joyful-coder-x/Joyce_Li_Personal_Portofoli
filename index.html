Part 1: Your 1st Print: “Customized Nametag”

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

Our first project is a name tag that can later become an access/promotion item for the 3D printers on campus. After about half a year of not touching the printers, this project was a great review of the process for me. Also, since my print went fairly smoothly, I was able to help some of my classmates. These experiences helped me learn quickly by seeing what can cause failure. For example, I now have a better grasp of how hard I can pull on stuck filament, how to tell if filament is too moist, etc. I reviewed how to set up the printer, feed the filament properly until the Prusa MINI+ machine "grabbed" it, and how to switch out colors. I also really like my final result—it fits perfectly with my backpack.

Detailed Notes and Images:

a. I forgot to unzip the file at first when importing the print, and it responded with an error. I was able to quickly fix it after reviewing my file and seeing that it was a .zip. Next time, I'll make sure to delete the zip file right after unzipping it.

b. My print ended up having a brim on the first layer (shown in Image 2). This is most likely because I clicked "Brim" without really remembering what it does. Looking back at the preview screenshot, it also shows the brim. I used a cutter and a file to get rid of the unwanted rim at the end.

c. In the end, the name tag had a few dark spots. I think this is because some stringing filament may have touched the nozzle and burned. I'm not sure if this is completely avoidable. Maybe slowing down the print could improve detail—this might reduce stringing, or it could make it worse by increasing the chance of burning. I want to test this more in the future.

Part 2: Second Print: A calibration “Torture Test”

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

This is Part 2 of our first project, focused on testing the limits of the printer by printing shapes that often cause failures. For this print, I used a pre-built torture test that I found online. I used the same settings as in Part 1.1, including the same material, nozzle size, layer height, and general print speed (as shown in the screenshot). This test helped me better understand the limitations of the printer, such as limits in unsupported parts, low-angle overhangs, and fine detail line work.

Detailed notes and improvements:

a. One side of the bridge test element detached from the build plate and was dragged slightly as the friction from the nozzle became greater than the adhesion at the bottom. This resulted in a mass of stringing. I think this may be related to the use of the brim feature that I accidentally used in Part 1.1, where increased surface area also affected how parts detached. However, a better solution would likely be to avoid this geometry or ensure stronger first-layer adhesion rather than relying on a brim.

b. Everything else printed fairly smoothly. There was some loss of detail in small holes and angled features, which shows that I should avoid very fine detail at steep angles, or choose models that allow for slower and more delicate printing when detail is important.

Overall, this test helped me better understand the practical limits of the printer and how those limits should influence my design choices in future projects.

Remixing 3D Designs
with Tinkercad and OnShape

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

Problem & Motivation
I currently do not use a soap holder and instead place my soap on a flat plate, which collects water and causes the soap to soften, crack, and become unpleasant to use. Many commercial soap holders have similar issues: they trap water underneath and require frequent dumping and cleaning. This repeated inconvenience motivated me to design a soap holder that could automatically direct water back into the sink while keeping both the soap and sink clean.

Initial Remix (Tinkercad)
I began by remixing an existing public soap holder model in Tinkercad. My main modification was adding a sloped bottom to encourage water to flow out on its own rather than pooling under the soap. While this improved drainage inside the holder, I discovered that the wall opening was still too high and the connection between the slope and the box was not smooth. As a result, water continued to collect underneath the holder instead of fully draining.

Redesign & Iteration (OnShape)
To address these issues, I redesigned the soap holder in OnShape by rebuilding the box geometry entirely. I added two pairs of uneven feet to elevate and tilt the entire holder so gravity could assist drainage more effectively. This second version successfully redirected water into the sink, but the internal web structure became unstable due to limited contact near the drainage opening.

Final Version & Outcome
In the third iteration, I added two additional edge supports to stabilize the web while maintaining the drainage path. This final version balances stability and function: the soap remains dry, water drains reliably into the sink, and the holder no longer collects residue underneath. Compared to my original setup and the remixed model, this design significantly reduces mess and eliminates the need for manual water removal.

Project 3.1: ROBOT BUILD & INITIAL ANALYSIS

261ENGR-11A-1: Introduction to Design Methodology

Project 3.1 documents the initial robot build process and the first round of form-and-fit analysis before designing the chassis.

Robot Assembly

We began by assembling the robot kit and soldering the electronics components. This process helped us understand how the robot works and where important components are located, including the wheels, sensors, LEDs, battery pack, and power button.

Building the robot first was important because the chassis design depends on the physical layout of the electronics and moving parts. After assembly, I laid out all the components to understand the structure of the robot and identify which areas would require openings or clearances in the enclosure.

Initial Design Considerations

Before designing the chassis, I analyzed the key functional requirements of the robot:

  • The wheels must move freely without obstruction
  • The sensors must be able to see the ground
  • The LED indicators should remain visible
  • The battery must be accessible for replacement
  • The power button must be reachable

Understanding these constraints helped guide the form and layout of the chassis design in the following stages.

Measurements & Fit Analysis

To design the enclosure accurately, I measured important parts of the robot using digital calipers and referenced those measurements while designing in CAD. These measurements defined the internal dimensions of the chassis and the placement of openings for the wheels, sensors, and button.

This step was critical because even small measurement errors can cause the chassis to not fit the robot correctly.

3D Printed Chassis

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

Version 2

Project 3.2 focuses on designing and iterating a 3D printed chassis enclosure for the robot.

Version 1 – Initial 3D Printed Chassis

My first 3D printed version was the pink chassis. In this version I tried to closely match the shape of the robot base so the chassis would fit almost one-to-one with the existing structure.

However, this version had two issues:

  • The overall length of the chassis was slightly too short
  • The square cutout in the middle did not align properly with the power button

These problems showed that designing too tightly around the exact shape of the base made the model harder to adjust.

Version 2 – Simplified Chassis Design

After discussing with another group, I realized the chassis did not need to perfectly follow the shape of the robot base. Instead, I simplified the design by switching to a rectangular enclosure.

In this version I:

  • increased the overall length of the chassis
  • removed the unnecessary middle square extension
  • simplified the internal structure

This version is the purple chassis, which successfully fits my robot.

Team Variation

Our team also created another version (blue chassis) to fit my partner's robot. Their robot had a slightly higher red LED light therefor need a higher hole for it.

Through this iterative process, the final 3D printed chassis successfully protected the electronics while allowing access to the wheels, sensors, and button.

Laser Cut Chassis

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

3.3fincal version

Project 3.3 explores designing the same robot chassis using laser cutting instead of 3D printing.

First Laser Cut Design

For my first laser cut design, I used the geometry from the 3D printed chassis and recreated it as a laser-cut structure with flat panels that could be assembled together.

However, the first version had three major errors:

  • I forgot to include the opening for the wheels.
  • I forgot to include the hole for the power button.
  • I accidentally left a cutting line along the edge, which caused part of the panel to be cut off when it was not supposed to.

These mistakes showed how important it is to carefully check cutting paths when preparing files for laser manufacturing.

Second Laser Cut Version

After correcting those issues, I produced a second laser cut chassis (the rectangular version on the left without tape). This version assembled correctly and successfully enclosed the robot.

However, after testing it I realized that the internal height was still slightly too low for the electronics components. Interestingly, the first laser cut version also had this issue, but I had not noticed it at the time.

Final Version

My partner also created a laser cut version for her robot. Her design fit her robot well, and by comparing both of our versions I was able to adjust the dimensions to create a final chassis that fits mine.

In the final version I also experimented with adding small decorative features to make the chassis resemble the shape of a car. In the future, I would like to add engraved drawings or patterns using the laser cutter to further improve the visual design.

Through this process I learned that laser cutting requires a different design approach than 3D printing, especially in terms of assembly structure, panel layout, and material thickness.

4.1: Ecosystem Review

Design Project #4: Open-source Assistive Tech

This section compares three major open-source assistive technology platforms to understand their focus, device range, and practical use context.

Platform Summary Devices Available
e-NABLE Hub A community-driven platform where volunteers and users collaborate to design, share, and improve assistive devices, with strong emphasis on connection and real-world use. Mainly human-powered prosthetic hands and arms (such as Phoenix Hand), with some upper-limb variations and a narrower scope outside prosthetics.
NIH 3D Print Exchange A structured, database-style repository for biomedical and assistive 3D models, focused on reliable file sharing rather than community interaction. Wide range of prosthetic and biomedical models; more diverse than e-NABLE but still technical and less focused on everyday assistive tools.
Makers Making Change A user-centered assistive technology catalog that connects people with disabilities to practical daily-use solutions through open designs. Diverse assistive tools beyond prosthetics, including grip aids, writing supports, and accessibility tools for daily tasks.

Quick comparison: e-NABLE emphasizes community and prosthetics, NIH emphasizes organized technical files, and Makers Making Change emphasizes user-centered everyday assistive devices.

4.5 Part 5: Reflect, Contribute Back, & Bridge to the Capstone

Design Project #4: Open-source Assistive Tech

Ethical Reflection:

In this project, we created directions for building the hand by using IDEO's "Solve for One, Extend to Many." We aimed to teach future classes how to build this hand and pass on tips that make the process faster and more efficient. This may allow more hands to be built and increase the chance of people in need getting help.

This also fits Norman's principle to improve design through testing and feedback. We first built it ourselves to test the current description, then wrote a small prototype, ran tests, and fixed and improved it for the final version.

In this way, both builders and possible future users can benefit from a more precise description. At the same time, builders who want to use other elements, sizes, or tools may be left out. Also, volunteers may not have enough medical knowledge and may require the disabled person to clearly explain their needs.

Bridge to the Capstone:

I found that the wrist-bending action used to close the hand takes a lot of force and is hard even for students with full hand function. I want to design a mechanism that needs less force to make the hand functional, possibly a leverage or pulley system. This is important because it can become an everyday tool, and making it more energy efficient will benefit users greatly.

Stage 1: Problem Definition & Brainstorming

Design Project #5: Accessible Delivery Hub

1. Research & Problem Framing
Clearly Defined Problem Statement

Right now, food delivery at Brandeis is a double-blind problem: drivers cannot find the right doors because of bad GPS pins, and students with disabilities cannot find their food because it is left in random spots on the ground. We are designing a Physical Delivery Hub that uses tactile markers and lighting to create a predictable, accessible drop-off point. Our goal is to bridge the gap between digital navigation and the built environment so students can get their dinner independently.

Background Research on the Problem and Existing Solutions

We focused on the physics of how humans and machines perceive and interact with the built environment. Since the project is about making a Delivery Hub findable and reachable, the main research areas are optics, haptics, ergonomics, and electronics.

  1. The Science of Visibility (Optics): A high light-reflectance difference helps students with low vision distinguish the hub from dark Brandeis pavement. We used the ADA 70% contrast standard as a guide and looked at highway signage as a model for readable white-on-dark visual contrast.
  2. The Science of Touch (Haptics): Tactile markers and Braille-style cues can help users navigate the hub by feel instead of sight. Detectable warning tiles were a useful reference because they communicate location through texture.
  3. The Science of Reach (Ergonomics): Anthropometrics helps define a safe reach zone for wheelchair users. A shelf height of 34 inches keeps the pickup area within the 15-to-48-inch side reach range used in ATMs and elevator controls.
  4. The Science of the Dark (Electronics): A light dependent resistor can automate safety lighting. Solar garden lights were a useful open-source electronics reference because they sense darkness and activate illumination automatically.

Existing solutions and features reviewed:

  1. Signage systems: Green and white signs create strong contrast during the day, while lit signage improves visibility at night.
  2. Reflective tape: White reflective tape makes the hub easier to spot after dark and strengthens the high-contrast edge condition.
  3. Insulated box materials: Heated blanket materials, foil, and Mylar were reviewed for keeping food warmer for longer.
  4. Elevated net shelf system: A raised netted base helps keep bags out of puddles or spills while maintaining airflow and structure.
  5. Waterproof outer curtain: A weather-protection layer can shield deliveries from rain and improve reliability across outdoor campus locations.

exist box

These references were the most useful because they contributed directly to visibility, accessibility, and protection from weather, which became the core priorities for the final concept.

Customer Profile

Our primary users are approximately 1,300 Brandeis students who navigate campus with physical or sensory disabilities, representing about 19% of the student population according to national NCES data. Their daily routine includes classes, social activities, and extracurriculars, and they often rely on food delivery when dining options are limited. The problem becomes most urgent from 6:00 PM to 9:00 PM, when low light, poor pin accuracy, and inaccessible drop-off locations make food retrieval difficult or unsafe. These users are most likely to be found near high-density transit points such as the SCC, Farber Library, and residential quads including Massell and North.

Design Thinking Methods Used

We used thematic analysis, insight statements, and How Might We ideation to frame the problem and expand solution directions. During team meetings, we first shared individual observations, grouped them into themes, then converted those insights into targeted HMW questions. We also used structured brainstorming guided by IDEO rules such as deferring judgment, encouraging wild ideas, and building on others' ideas.

Team Collaboration Agreement

Meetings are held every Sunday from 11:00 AM to 12:00 PM.

2. Problem Definition & Brainstorming
Thematic Analysis Process and Resulting Themes

We documented brainstorming ideas on a shared whiteboard, then grouped them into larger design directions. Three major themes emerged: lighting and electronics for nighttime visibility, physical structure and materials for accessibility and protection, and navigation cues for drivers and students.

  • Theme A: Lighting and electronics for visibility at night
  • Theme B: Physical structure and materials for shelf access, insulation, and weather protection
  • Theme C: Navigation cues for driver wayfinding and accurate drop-off location
Insight Statements
  1. Students using wheelchairs need a waist-height interface to retrieve delivered food safely.
  2. Students with mobility challenges need nearby and predictable delivery locations.
  3. Drivers struggle to locate correct entrances because GPS pin placement is often inaccurate.
  4. Students with low vision struggle to locate deliveries in low-light conditions.
  5. Food left on the ground creates both accessibility barriers and safety risks.
How Might We Statements
  1. How might we ensure someone in a wheelchair can access delivered food easily?
  2. How might we make delivery locations easy for drivers to find using both GPS and physical cues?
  3. How might we make a delivery drop-off point visible in low-light conditions?
  4. How might we create a system that works for both drivers and students simultaneously?
  5. How might we prevent food from being placed in inaccessible or unsafe locations?
Brainstorming Process

We conducted a structured brainstorming session on a whiteboard, following IDEO guidance to generate a large set of ideas before evaluating them. After ideation, we grouped concepts by theme and selected the most promising directions based on desirability, feasibility, and viability.

Top Ideas
  1. Illuminated signage system: A green and white sign with reflective tape and automatic lighting for stronger visibility during the day and at night.
  2. Insulated delivery box: A protected structure using insulation materials to help maintain food temperature.
  3. Elevated net shelf system: A multi-layer shelf with a raised netted base to keep food off wet or dirty ground.
  4. Weather-protected hub: A waterproof outer curtain that helps shield deliveries from rain and other environmental conditions.
Final Selected Idea with Rationale

We selected a small delivery hub with illuminated signage, tactile features, and a protected shelf system because it best balances desirability, feasibility, and viability. It supports both driver navigation and student accessibility, and it builds directly on open-source references that can realistically be adapted with available materials and time.

3. Storyboard

The storyboard documents a ten-frame user journey showing both the delivery driver and the student interacting with the system.

  1. A delivery driver receives an order assigned to a specific delivery station.
  2. The driver checks the pin for the delivery station and navigates to campus.
  3. The driver locates the delivery station using visible signage.
  4. The driver places the food inside the delivery station.
  5. The delivery station protects the food from the elements.
  6. The recipient checks the app and sees the order has been delivered.
  7. The recipient locates the delivery station using signage and the Google Maps pin.
  8. The recipient travels to the delivery station independently.
  9. The recipient finds the correct delivery through the sorting system.
  10. The recipient collects the food and returns to their dorm or library space.

This storyboard starts with the original accessibility problem and shows how the hub improves the delivery experience for both drivers and students across campus.

Stage 2: Need Define and Prototype

Design Project #5: Accessible Delivery Hub

Needs Statement

Brandeis students with physical and visual disabilities need a standardized, waist-height delivery interface because current ground-level drop-offs create physical hazards and navigation barriers.

2-4 Design Principles

These are the features our prototypes must have to be successful.

  1. Accessibility: The shelf surface must be between 15 and 48 inches from the ground to meet ADA reach standards.
  2. Visibility: The unit must use high-contrast colors (70% contrast) to be visible in the 6:00 PM to 9:00 PM "critical window."
  3. Tactility: The design must include physical textures so it can be identified by touch alone.
What Would Success Look Like?

Visibility Test

  • The Metric: The maximum distance (in feet) a person can correctly identify the sign or tactile icons in the dark (6:00 PM – 9:00 PM).
  • Goal: Increase the detection distance by 50% between Cycle 1 and Cycle 4.
  • How to iterate: If Cycle 1 (small text) is only visible from 2 feet, Cycle 2 (larger CAD geometry) should aim for 5 feet.

The "Tactile Recognition" Test (Usability)

  • The Metric: The time (in seconds) it takes a user to identify the "Delivery Hub" icon using only their hands.
  • Goal: Reduce identification time.
  • How to iterate: If the texture in Cycle 1 is too shallow, Cycle 2 should change the CAD to make the bumps taller or sharper.

My team and I plan to test visibility and usability by having Sandra place a food delivery order to the location using the pin we created. We will also put up our sign at the location. If the delivery driver is able to find the spot and leave the order on the designated shelf, that will indicate the system is working properly. If not, we will make the necessary adjustments and refine our approach.

Design Approach

For our sketch, we created a 3D-printable sign that directly addresses our Needs Statement by transforming a standard shelf into an accessible delivery hub. The primary feature of the design is the large, centered "DROP HERE" text paired with recognizable food and beverage icons. This visual and tactile layout is designed to be immediately intuitive for delivery drivers, ensuring they place orders at the correct, waist-height level rather than on the ground.

To meet our Design Principles, the sign includes the following specific features:

  • Tactility: The text and icons are designed as raised 3D geometries, allowing students with visual impairments to identify the hub through touch alone.
  • Accessibility: By designing a mounting system that fits our specific 3-tier shelf, we ensure the sign is positioned between 15 and 48 inches from the ground, meeting ADA reach standards.
  • Visibility: The high-contrast layout of the icons is intended to be recognizable even in low-light "critical windows" between 6:00 PM and 9:00 PM.
Iteration Cycles Overview
Cycle 1: Print the first version in PLA

For our first print (Cycle 1), we used Lightburn to laser cut our sign. This was basically our test to see if the size was right and if the clips actually stayed on the Amazon shelf.

What we learned from testing: We ran a couple of quick tests to get some numbers. We checked Visibility Distance (how far away you can read it) around 3.5 feet in low light and Tactile Recognition (how fast you can identify the icons by touch).

  • The Good: The sign looked great and fit the shelf perfectly.
  • The Bad: The tactile bumps were a little too flat. It took way too long to "read" the sign with just our hands because the plastic was too smooth.
  • The Pivot: For Cycle 2, we're going back into CAD to make those tactile icons much taller and sharper so they're easier to feel.
Cycle 2: Change the shape (CAD)

For our second version, we shifted from the flat laser-cut baseline to a more functional assembly to solve the mounting and usability issues we found in Cycle 1. We also played around with the shape to make something newer. The original laser-cut sign didn't have a secure way to attach to the Amazon shelf (which we also built during this cycle), and the flat surface didn't provide enough tactile feedback for someone to "read" the icons by hand.

The Key CAD Changes:

  • Zip Tie Integration: We added specific mounting holes directly into the CAD design. This allows us to use zip ties to lock the sign onto the shelf bars, making it much more stable so it doesn't wiggle or fall off when a student or delivery driver touches it.
  • Tactile Pop: We redesigned the icons and text to have significant depth. This makes the sign much easier to identify through touch alone, meeting our design principle for tactility.
  • Material Shift: We moved away from a single flat piece to this layered assembly to increase the overall durability and visual contrast of the hub.

Testing & Results: We used our consistent testing methodology to see if these physical changes actually moved the needle on our success criteria.

  • Visibility Distance: Our detection distance jumped from 3.5 feet (Cycle 1) to 5.5 feet since we added some visual contrast.
  • Tactile Recognition Time: Adding that physical depth made a huge difference. In our usability tests, the time it took to identify the "Delivery Hub" icon by hand was cut in half compared to the flat Cycle 1 version.
  • Mounting Success: The new zip-tie holes successfully secured the sign at the required waist-height (between 15 and 48 inches), ensuring the hub stays accessible and in place for every delivery.
Cycle 3: Change the CAM settings

In Cycle 3, we kept our CAD design from Cycle 2 the same but focused entirely on our CAM settings within LightBurn. Our goal was to refine the surface of the sign to make the tactile elements more professional and easier to identify by touch.

The Key CAM Changes:

  • Switch to Engraving Mode: Instead of just cutting out shapes, we utilized the engraving feature in LightBurn. This allowed us to "etch" the food icons and "DROP HERE" text directly into the material with varying depths.
  • Scan Parameter Adjustments: We adjusted the Line Interval and DPI settings in the Cut Settings Editor to create a smoother, more consistent finish. By using a high-quality dithering mode like Stucki, we ensured the transition between the engraved icons and the base board was sharp and distinct.
  • Precision Power Control: We fine-tuned the laser's Max and Min Power settings to ensure the engraving was deep enough to be felt without scorching the acrylic base.

Testing & Results: Using our consistent quantitative methodology, we measured how these machine-level changes affected the sign's performance.

  • Tactile Recognition Time: The switch to engraving was a game-changer for usability. Because the engraved surface has a consistent, slightly "toothed" texture compared to the smooth plastic stickers from Cycle 2, users were able to identify the icons by touch significantly faster.
  • Visibility Distance: The engraving added a layer of visual "matte" finish to the icons, which reduced glare in the 6:00 PM – 9:00 PM "critical window". This pushed our visibility distance from 5.5 feet to 6.2 feet.
  • Durability: Unlike the stickers used in Cycle 2, which could peel off over time, the engraved design is a permanent part of the board, ensuring the hub remains accessible for the long term.
Cycle 4: Change the material

For our final iteration, we made the biggest change yet by switching our base material from wood to acrylic. While the wood in Cycle 3 was great for testing our engraving settings, it didn't provide the professional look or the high-contrast visibility we needed for a campus delivery hub.

The Key Material Changes:

  • Wood to Acrylic: We moved away from the wooden base and used a thick acrylic board. This change makes the sign weather-resistant for campus use and gives it a much cleaner, more modern look.
  • High-Contrast Layering: By combining the acrylic with our design overlays, we achieved the 70% color contrast required by our design principles.
  • Light Refraction: The acrylic base catches ambient light much better than wood, which is a huge help during the 6:00 PM – 9:00 PM "critical window" when visibility is lowest.

Testing & Results (Final Score): We ran our final round of quantitative tests to see how the material change impacted our success criteria.

  • Visibility Distance: This was our biggest win. The contrast of the green on acrylic allowed our testers to identify the "Drop Here" sign from 9 feet away. This is a massive jump from our 3.5-foot baseline in Cycle 1, easily beating our goal of a 50% increase.
  • Tactile Recognition Time: The engraved acrylic felt even "sharper" than the engraved wood, allowing users to identify the food icons by touch in record time.
  • Durability: The acrylic is much tougher and won't warp or degrade if it gets hit by rain or snow, making it a "repair the world" solution that will actually last on campus.
Final Conclusion

Moving from a flat laser-cut sign to an engraved acrylic hub, we successfully created a tool that makes deliveries accessible for everyone. Our Dozuki Guide will not be about how to "assemble an Amazon shelf". It will be a guide on how to 3D print and install the accessibility upgrade kit we designed so that any shelf can be turned into an Accessible Delivery Hub.
