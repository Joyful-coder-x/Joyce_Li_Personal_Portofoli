Part 1: Your 1st Print: “Customized Nametag”

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

Our first project is a name tag that can later become an access/promotion item for the 3D printers on campus. After about half a year of not touching the printers, this project was a great review of the process for me. Also, since my print went fairly smoothly, I was able to help some of my classmates. These experiences helped me learn quickly by seeing what can cause failure. For example, I now have a better grasp of how hard I can pull on stuck filament, how to tell if filament is too moist, etc. I reviewed how to set up the printer, feed the filament properly until the Prusa MINI+ machine "grabbed" it, and how to switch out colors. I also really like my final result—it fits perfectly with my backpack.

Detailed Notes and Images:

a. I forgot to unzip the file at first when importing the print, and it responded with an error. I was able to quickly fix it after reviewing my file and seeing that it was a .zip. Next time, I'll make sure to delete the zip file right after unzipping it.

b. My print ended up having a brim on the first layer (shown in Image 2). This is most likely because I clicked "Brim" without really remembering what it does. Looking back at the preview screenshot, it also shows the brim. I used a cutter and a file to get rid of the unwanted rim at the end.

c. In the end, the name tag had a few dark spots. I think this is because some stringing filament may have touched the nozzle and burned. I'm not sure if this is completely avoidable. Maybe slowing down the print could improve detail—this might reduce stringing, or it could make it worse by increasing the chance of burning. I want to test this more in the future.

Part 2: Second Print: A calibration “Torture Test”

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

This is Part 2 of our first project, focused on testing the limits of the printer by printing shapes that often cause failures. For this print, I used a pre-built torture test that I found online. I used the same settings as in Part 1.1, including the same material, nozzle size, layer height, and general print speed (as shown in the screenshot). This test helped me better understand the limitations of the printer, such as limits in unsupported parts, low-angle overhangs, and fine detail line work.

Detailed notes and improvements:

a. One side of the bridge test element detached from the build plate and was dragged slightly as the friction from the nozzle became greater than the adhesion at the bottom. This resulted in a mass of stringing. I think this may be related to the use of the brim feature that I accidentally used in Part 1.1, where increased surface area also affected how parts detached. However, a better solution would likely be to avoid this geometry or ensure stronger first-layer adhesion rather than relying on a brim.

b. Everything else printed fairly smoothly. There was some loss of detail in small holes and angled features, which shows that I should avoid very fine detail at steep angles, or choose models that allow for slower and more delicate printing when detail is important.

Overall, this test helped me better understand the practical limits of the printer and how those limits should influence my design choices in future projects.

Part 3: Your 3rd Print: “Open Source Assistive Devices”

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

This is the final part of our first project. For this part, with a basic grasp of the abilities and limitations of 3D printing, we moved forward to looking at how it can be used in real life. We looked through Makers Making Change and printed one device that customizes a household item to better fit accessibility needs. This shows one of the main strengths of 3D printing: its ability to quickly create low-cost, customized, and functional solutions for specific users.

For this print, I chose a knife assistance device that helps people with weak hands or who are unable to grasp properly by providing an extra point to apply pressure. I used the same settings as in Part 1.1, including the same material, nozzle size, layer height, and general print speed (as shown in the screenshot).

Quick Note: I do not have a photo of the print on my knife at home yet. I will add it later today and submit an updated version.

Detailed Notes and Improvements:

a. Due to a lack of adjustment in the measurements, the magnets were slightly off. I fixed this by gluing them in place, but it would be better to adjust the design based on my specific measurements next time.

b. My smaller knife does not work very well with this handle, since the blade width is a bit short and my hand starts to get in the way. This shows that the design works better for certain knife sizes and should be resized or modified for smaller tools.

Remixing 3D Designs
with Tinkercad and OnShape

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

Problem & Motivation
I currently do not use a soap holder and instead place my soap on a flat plate, which collects water and causes the soap to soften, crack, and become unpleasant to use. Many commercial soap holders have similar issues: they trap water underneath and require frequent dumping and cleaning. This repeated inconvenience motivated me to design a soap holder that could automatically direct water back into the sink while keeping both the soap and sink clean.

Initial Remix (Tinkercad)
I began by remixing an existing public soap holder model in Tinkercad. My main modification was adding a sloped bottom to encourage water to flow out on its own rather than pooling under the soap. While this improved drainage inside the holder, I discovered that the wall opening was still too high and the connection between the slope and the box was not smooth. As a result, water continued to collect underneath the holder instead of fully draining.

Redesign & Iteration (OnShape)
To address these issues, I redesigned the soap holder in OnShape by rebuilding the box geometry entirely. I added two pairs of uneven feet to elevate and tilt the entire holder so gravity could assist drainage more effectively. This second version successfully redirected water into the sink, but the internal web structure became unstable due to limited contact near the drainage opening.

Final Version & Outcome
In the third iteration, I added two additional edge supports to stabilize the web while maintaining the drainage path. This final version balances stability and function: the soap remains dry, water drains reliably into the sink, and the holder no longer collects residue underneath. Compared to my original setup and the remixed model, this design significantly reduces mess and eliminates the need for manual water removal.

Project 3.1: ROBOT BUILD & INITIAL ANALYSIS

261ENGR-11A-1: Introduction to Design Methodology

Project 3.1 documents the initial robot build process and the first round of form-and-fit analysis before designing the chassis.

Robot Assembly

We began by assembling the robot kit and soldering the electronics components. This process helped us understand how the robot works and where important components are located, including the wheels, sensors, LEDs, battery pack, and power button.

Building the robot first was important because the chassis design depends on the physical layout of the electronics and moving parts. After assembly, I laid out all the components to understand the structure of the robot and identify which areas would require openings or clearances in the enclosure.

Initial Design Considerations

Before designing the chassis, I analyzed the key functional requirements of the robot:

  • The wheels must move freely without obstruction
  • The sensors must be able to see the ground
  • The LED indicators should remain visible
  • The battery must be accessible for replacement
  • The power button must be reachable

Understanding these constraints helped guide the form and layout of the chassis design in the following stages.

Measurements & Fit Analysis

To design the enclosure accurately, I measured important parts of the robot using digital calipers and referenced those measurements while designing in CAD. These measurements defined the internal dimensions of the chassis and the placement of openings for the wheels, sensors, and button.

This step was critical because even small measurement errors can cause the chassis to not fit the robot correctly.

3D Printed Chassis

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

Version 2

Project 3.2 focuses on designing and iterating a 3D printed chassis enclosure for the robot.

Version 1 – Initial 3D Printed Chassis

My first 3D printed version was the pink chassis. In this version I tried to closely match the shape of the robot base so the chassis would fit almost one-to-one with the existing structure.

However, this version had two issues:

  • The overall length of the chassis was slightly too short
  • The square cutout in the middle did not align properly with the power button

These problems showed that designing too tightly around the exact shape of the base made the model harder to adjust.

Version 2 – Simplified Chassis Design

After discussing with another group, I realized the chassis did not need to perfectly follow the shape of the robot base. Instead, I simplified the design by switching to a rectangular enclosure.

In this version I:

  • increased the overall length of the chassis
  • removed the unnecessary middle square extension
  • simplified the internal structure

This version is the purple chassis, which successfully fits my robot.

Team Variation

Our team also created another version (blue chassis) to fit my partner's robot. Their robot had a slightly higher red LED light therefor need a higher hole for it.

Through this iterative process, the final 3D printed chassis successfully protected the electronics while allowing access to the wheels, sensors, and button.

Laser Cut Chassis

261ENGR-11A-1 : Introduction to Design Methodology

3.3fincal version

Project 3.3 explores designing the same robot chassis using laser cutting instead of 3D printing.

First Laser Cut Design

For my first laser cut design, I used the geometry from the 3D printed chassis and recreated it as a laser-cut structure with flat panels that could be assembled together.

However, the first version had three major errors:

  • I forgot to include the opening for the wheels.
  • I forgot to include the hole for the power button.
  • I accidentally left a cutting line along the edge, which caused part of the panel to be cut off when it was not supposed to.

These mistakes showed how important it is to carefully check cutting paths when preparing files for laser manufacturing.

Second Laser Cut Version

After correcting those issues, I produced a second laser cut chassis (the rectangular version on the left without tape). This version assembled correctly and successfully enclosed the robot.

However, after testing it I realized that the internal height was still slightly too low for the electronics components. Interestingly, the first laser cut version also had this issue, but I had not noticed it at the time.

Final Version

My partner also created a laser cut version for her robot. Her design fit her robot well, and by comparing both of our versions I was able to adjust the dimensions to create a final chassis that fits mine.

In the final version I also experimented with adding small decorative features to make the chassis resemble the shape of a car. In the future, I would like to add engraved drawings or patterns using the laser cutter to further improve the visual design.

Through this process I learned that laser cutting requires a different design approach than 3D printing, especially in terms of assembly structure, panel layout, and material thickness.
